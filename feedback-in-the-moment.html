The Challenge

Feedback was only happening during formal reviews

Netflix's Learning & People Development team had a problem: employees were only engaging with feedback during formal 360 review cycles. Outside of those windows, everyday feedback — the kind that actually shifts behavior — wasn't happening.

Their existing resources weren't solving it. The Feedback Workshop was a one-size-fits-all session. The Feedback Toolkit was comprehensive but overwhelming. Neither was meeting people in the flow of their actual work.

The ask: design a single digital learning experience — 5 to 7 minutes, mobile-responsive, hosted in Docebo — that helps employees build real feedback habits across four global regions with meaningfully different cultural norms.

Research & Insight

The gap isn't knowledge — it's action

Netflix provided sentiment data from employees across all four regions. A few patterns stood out: engagement with feedback tools dropped sharply outside of 360 cycles, employees consistently reported a gap between wanting to give feedback and actually doing it, and the reasons varied by person — not just by region. A new hire in London and a director in São Paulo might avoid feedback for completely different reasons, or for the same one.

Before designing anything, I mapped out the honest scope of what a learning experience could move — and what it couldn't.

What training can change

  • Employees lack a simple, repeatable approach for in-the-moment feedback
  • People need practice reps in realistic, culturally nuanced contexts
  • Adapting feedback style across relationships, power dynamics, and cultural norms

What needs support from other parts of the business

  • Feedback infrastructure that sustains beyond 360 cycles
  • Managers modeling the behavior — asking for feedback, not just giving it
  • Feedback woven into existing rituals like 1:1s, retros, and milestones

This distinction matters. A 5-minute digital experience won't single-handedly shift a culture. But it can be the strongest lever L&D owns — if we're honest about the dependencies.

Design Approach

A four-week system, not a single course

Instead of another workshop or toolkit, I designed a four-week spaced learning system delivered through Slack into Docebo. Each week, a notification drops the learner into a single 5-to-7-minute scenario-based module:

1

Week 1: Give It

Initiating feedback in a real moment — even when it feels uncomfortable.

2

Week 2: Receive It

Hearing hard feedback without shutting down or getting defensive.

3

Week 3: Seek It

Actively asking for the feedback you're not getting.

4

Week 4: Do It

Self-directed practice — a real conversation, supported but not scaffolded.

Why this structure

Spaced over time, not all at once. A single session doesn't build a habit. Weekly pacing gives learners time to practice in the real world between modules.

Scenario-based, not theoretical. Every module anchors to a recognizable workplace moment. Learners practice making choices, not reading about them.

Adaptive by barrier, not by region. At the start of each module, a check-in asks learners to self-identify their biggest barrier. The experience routes them into the scenario path that matches. A tenured employee frustrated by past attempts gets a different experience than a new hire who doesn't feel they have standing yet.

Bridges to real action. Every module ends with a concrete commitment — something the learner will actually do that week. The learning isn't complete when the module ends. It's complete when they have the conversation.

The Experience

Module 1: Give It — Following Margo's path

The prototype is a fully working interactive experience built in React. It follows Margo, an EMEA new hire who's excited about Netflix's feedback culture but feels too new to speak up.

1

Check-In

Two quick questions surface the learner's barrier and route them to the right scenario path. No region selector, no role assignment — the learner tells us what's holding them back.

2

Setup

A clip from Emily in Paris sets the scene. Emily — a new hire in a foreign office — has feedback for her creative director but isn't sure she has standing. The scene is entertaining, but it's doing real work: normalizing the feeling of being new and having something to say.

3

Practice

The learner works through Emily's situation step by step, assembling feedback using the I/B/I/A framework (Intention, Behavior, Impact, Action). At each step, they choose from options and get immediate coaching — not just right or wrong, but why one approach lands better.

4

Takeaway

The learner writes their own real feedback script using what they practiced, then commits to one conversation they'll have this week. The module doesn't end with a quiz score. It ends with a plan.

Key Design Decisions

Four choices that shaped the experience

Netflix IP instead of generic scenarios

Netflix has a content library no other company has. Using clips from Emily in Paris makes the experience feel like a Netflix product, not a training module. It gives learners an emotional hook that stock scenarios never could.

Slack delivery instead of LMS notifications

Slack is where Netflix employees already work. Meeting learners there means feedback practice doesn't feel like a separate task. Docebo's native Slack integration makes this technically feasible, not just conceptually nice.

Weekly pacing instead of self-paced

A single session doesn't build a habit. Spacing it out — one module per week — gives learners time to actually have the real conversation before the next skill drops.

Self-selection instead of assigned paths

The check-in asks about the learner's barrier, and the experience adapts. A new hire in London and a director in São Paulo might select the same barrier — and get the same path, because their need is the same.

What I'd Do Next

This prototype is Module 1 of four

Complete the system. Build Modules 2 (Receive It), 3 (Seek It), and 4 (Do It) to the same depth. Each follows the same flow but focuses on a different feedback skill with its own scenarios and IP clips.

Partner with PM&O for sustainment. After Week 4, the experience transitions from structured modules to micro-prompts embedded in existing team rituals — standups, 1:1s, retros. This requires a real partnership with People Management & Operations to make feedback part of the operating system, not just the learning system.

Build the measurement layer. Track not just completion, but behavior change — are feedback conversations happening more often? Are barrier patterns shifting over time? The xAPI telemetry from Docebo would feed this, but the real signal comes from pairing learning data with sentiment data.

Refine through iteration. Run a pilot with one region, gather real learner data, and use it to improve the scenarios, pacing, and adaptive logic before scaling globally.
